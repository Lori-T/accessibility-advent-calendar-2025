Ensuring good colour contrast is one of the quickest ways to improve accessibility. Good contrast helps people with low vision, colour blindness or anyone reading content on a small screen or in bright light. It ensures text and interactive elements remain clear and readable.

You can find out more about how colour contrast affects people in Day 7 of last year's advent calendar - Understanding colour contrast (opens in a new tab).

What you’re checking today

You’re checking whether text and user interface components have enough contrast against their background. This helps users perceive content quickly and comfortably.

Quick 5-minute check

Follow these steps using tools built into your browser:

  • right-click any piece of text on your page and select Inspect to open developer tools
  • in the Styles panel, look for the color and background properties
  • click the coloured square beside the value to open the browser’s colour picker
  • check the displayed contrast ratio – Chrome and Firefox show whether it passes WCAG
  • if your browser does not show contrast information, copy the colours into a free checker like WebAIM Contrast Checker

The WCAG 2.2 skill behind this

1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) – text must have enough contrast with its background. WCAG requires:

  • 4.5:1 for normal text
  • 3:1 for large text (18pt or 14pt bold)
  • 3:1 for icons and user interface components

Why this matters

Low contrast is one of the most common accessibility issues. It makes reading difficult or impossible for many users and creates barriers on mobile devices or in bright environments.

Try this bonus check

Switch your device to dark mode and view your page again. Do text, buttons and links still meet contrast requirements?
